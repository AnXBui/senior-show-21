Cold Graphic

A look into the evolution of propoganda graphic during the Cold War.

A Digital Exhibition by An Bui

Scroll to Begin

A war fought in hearts and minds.

The cold war was one of the most culturally important periods of recent American and Russian history, and has much defined half of what we think of 20th century culture in many different ways. And much of that war was fought not with bombs and bullets but through the use of propaganda, and design played a huge role in shaping the public opinion on both sides. In this exhibition, we will explore how the graphic design was developed and evolved throughout the Cold War on both sides of the war. By examining the cultural and societal context of which these designs were created, we can reveal the methods and cultural motifs behind these works of state propaganda, including their influences on each other and impact on society.

Carrier, Join Navy

Joseph Binder

C. 1960

United States

Unframed Dimensions 27H X 19W

This “Join Navy” poster was part of a recruiting campaign by the U.S Navy during the Cold War. Here, we can see the Cubist and European Modernism influence coming through strongly through the bold composition and decisive choice of colors. Most likely inspired by the like of famous painter and designer A. J. Cassandre, this design employs stylized geometric forms in flat bold colors to create a strong sense of structure and unity. The emphasis on the imposing and dominating shape of the carrier—as well as its center position in the layout—reminds the viewer of the military strength and technological advantages of the U.S Navy.

Untitled

NSA, FOIA Case#83661

c.1950 — c.1970

United States

This poster is part of a National Security campaign initiated by the NSA during 1950s in an effort to combat Communist influence during the Cold War. The underlying text is borrowed from the famous speech made by President Lincoln during the Gettysburg Address, which is rendered in traditional serif typeface to instill austerity and historical importance. This is contrasted by the blood-like graffiti overlaid on top of it. The crossing of “under god" most likely references the anti-religion policy of the USSR, while the replacing of “people” with ‘state" is a reference to the perceived oppression of the Soviet government on its people. This is reflective of the collectivist agenda often found in USSR's propaganda which emphasizes collective good (in this case the benefits of the “State") over individual's (or “People's”) benefits. As this campaign was targeted at U.S officials, this message about the USSR and its extreme oppressive policies serves as a cautionary tale about the US's opposition in the Cold War. This also shed some light on the general attitude toward the USSR during this tenuous period of the Cold War.

Mrs. Behind the Missile

Scientific American

1958

United States

Magazine Ads

How do you advertise to hire a long-range missile designer during the Cold War? This ad for a Missile designer featured in the 1958 Scientific American magazine might be the answer: you “empower" the housewives. In this layered composition created by utilizing paint and photographic materials, we can see the focus is on the painted image of the ‘ideal American housewife’. Undoubtedly, this is targeted at white middle-class to upper-class housewives in an attempt to talk their husbands into joining the program. In aid of this, the composition also employ a dynamic layout and a bold red as accent to create a sense of ‘empowerment’ to its targeted female audience. Note that at the time, this “female empowerment" narrative was often seen as progressive and appealing to the general female audience.

First man on the moon stamp

U.S Postal Service

1969

United States

Printed Stamp

The Cold War between the superpowers extend far beyond Earth, and into space. As 1969 marked the first time a human set foot on the moon, the US Postal Service created this special edition stamp to commemorate the achievement by the NASA program. The two bold text creates a framing device for the central image, which is rendered in vivid details. Considering the landing was featured on live television in front of millions of audience, this illustrative and semi-realistic depiction was a way to remind the viewer of what they saw on television, and inspire a sense of American pride.

Communism Means Terrorism

U.S Information Agency

September 15th, 1954

United States

Printed Poster

The Vietnam War was one of the important proxy war between the United States and the USSR. The U.S, in an attempt to fend off Communist influence in Vietnam, created many propaganda posters targeted at the Vietnamese population, including this one. Here, we can see the vulture (most likely referring to the Soviet leadership) is the center of focus, and rendered in brutal and graphic detail. The color of red, which is code for the USSR, is used to paint a bloody picture which emphasizes the brutality of Communist ‘terrorists.’ Note that this poster is very descriptive in its imagery, and utilize iconography and symbolism to communicate the message effectively even without reading the text. This was vital since the Vietnamese population at the time was proportionally illiterate, and propaganda was an important part of guerrilla warfare.

1917 – The beginning of a new era. All power to the Soviets!

Sachkov

1988

Soviet Union

Printed Poster

This three-panel poster commemorates the Russian revolution of 1917. To emphasize the power and unity of the people, the citizen / soldiers are rendered stylistically and blend together to form a solid form to show a sense of cohesion and united strength. This narrative of the putting the good of the Collective over the personal's need is especially prevalent among Soviet media. The signature USSR color of red is also used generously in the top banner and dotted throughout the composition to demonstrate a singular will and a common goal. It also acts as a visual reminder of the influence and power of the Communist Party. To strengthen this idea, factories and a warship can be seen in the background, representing industrial and military might respectively.

A Mighty Sport Power

B. Reshetnikov

1962

Soviet Union

Printed Poster

The Olympics was a constant point of competition between the U.S and the USSR. In this poster, we can see how the USSR was showcasing the State’s various athletic achievements. With the gold Olympic medals as the background, this poster features a tall, muscular, idealized version of the USSR athlete, almost in parallel to the image of ‘Superman' or superheroes most commonly found in American media at the time. The classical painterly technique used to render this central figure, along with the bold thick-stroke typography, reinforces a sense of national pride and confidence. This also helps elevate and establish the status of the USSR athletes to that of heroes and mythical legends.

"I wouldn't suggest using force Mr. Reagan"

Unknown

1980

Soviet Union

Minsk History Museum, Belarus

This poster was created by the USSR as a critical response to the US's Reagan reform in 1980s. The composition focus on a big bold typography spelling out CCCP (Russian abbreviation for USSR) which is rendered in red bold solid color. In front of the text is a central strong-male figure which inspire a confidence and willpower in his posture, seemingly in a way to protect the USSR from the external influence of the capitalist West (hence why the CCCP is seen shielded behind the figure). The typography also help framed the figure in a radiating pattern, which accentuates the sense of power and authority.

Through the Worlds and Centuries

Miron Lukianov and Vasily Ostrovsky,

July 1965

Soviet Union

The Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics

Here we see another USSR poster which focuses on the space race. A stylized white road is overlay on top of an image of the stars, and walking on it is a red USSR soldier bearing the Soviet flag. This overlapping of Soviet graphic and space imagery reveal an intense ambition for space travel and reaching the stars. This is especially effective in combination with how the contrasting white road taper off into the trailing smoke of a launching rocket. The use of solid and concentrated red also inspire a sense of national pride and optimism, painting a future for the USSR that extends far beyond the stars.

In the name of peace

Iraklii Toidze

1959

Soviet Union

The Memorial Museum of Cosmonautics

This poster was originally featured as part of the 1989's San Diego exhibition of various USSR posters supported by the USSR government. This was an effort to reconcile the difference between the two superpowers, as the Cold War was coming to an end in 1991. The color blue, recognized by its association for peace and freedom, is used to inspire unity and an atmosphere of non-violence. The Roman god Mercury – known for his association with commerce and the messenger god – is shown to emphasized a future of US–USSR collaboration and mutual-benefit. The two flags of the two states are also rendered in similar style, and share the same hue of red, which show a singular will for peace and cooperation from both sides.

Youth Issue 4 Cover

V. Koltyar

1976

Soviet Union

Printed Magazine Cover

Moscow Design Museum

The space race was also a major narrative in the U.S.S.R , and was a major theme in many of its media. This design is for the USSR's Youth Magazine Issue 4, showing an image of a rocket blasting into space. Notice the vibrant color array being used as a motif throughout the composition (both in the blast and in the atmosphere). This paints an optimistic and hopeful future of USSR and emphasizes the dream of space travel.
